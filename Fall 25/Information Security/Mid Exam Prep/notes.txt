Part 1: Core Security Concepts
This section covers the fundamental definitions and goals of information security.
1.1 Key Definitions
 * Cyber Security: 
 The protection of information in a networked system of computers, devices, and
  transmission lines (including the Internet) .
 * Information Security:
  A subset of cybersecurity focused on preserving the confidentiality, integrity,
   and availability (CIA) of information .
    It can also include authenticity, accountability, 
    non-repudiation, and reliability .
 * Computer Security:
  A general term for tools that protect data and thwart hackers .
 * Network Security:
  Measures to protect data during transmission .
1.2 The CIA Triad (Security Objectives)
This is a central model for information security:
 * Confidentiality: Assures that private information is not disclosed to unauthorized individuals . This also covers Privacy, which is an individual's control over what information about them is collected and shared .
 * Integrity: Assures that data and programs are changed only in an authorized way (Data Integrity) and that a system performs its intended function without unauthorized manipulation (System Integrity) .
 * Availability: Assures that systems work promptly and service is not denied to authorized users .
1.3 Other Key Objectives
 * Authenticity: Being genuine and verifiable; having confidence in the validity of a message or originator .
 * Accountability: The ability to trace an entity's actions uniquely to that entity .
 * Non-repudiation: Provides proof of delivery to the sender and proof of the sender's identity to the recipient, so neither can deny processing the information .
1.4 Security Attacks, Mechanisms, and Services
 * Security Attack: Any action that compromises the security of an organization's information .
   * Passive Attacks: Involve monitoring transmissions to obtain information. They do not alter data.
   * Active Attacks: Involve modifying data or creating a false stream.
 * Security Service: A service that enhances the security of data processing and transfer, designed to counter security attacks . Examples include authentication , access control , and confidentiality.
 * Security Mechanism: A feature designed to detect, prevent, or recover from a security attack . A single mechanism can support multiple services. Many mechanisms rely on cryptographic techniques .
Part 2: Introduction to Cryptography
This section covers the basic terms and models of cryptography.
 * Cryptography: A branch of mathematics dealing with data transformation . It is essential for secure data storage and transmission .
 * Plaintext: The original, unencrypted message .
 * Ciphertext: The coded, encrypted message .
 * Encryption (Enciphering): The process of converting plaintext to ciphertext .
 * Decryption (Deciphering): The process of restoring plaintext from ciphertext .
 * Symmetric Cipher Model: A model where both sender and receiver share a single secret key . The decryption algorithm is essentially the reverse of the encryption algorithm .
2.1 Attack Approaches
 * Cryptanalysis: Techniques used to decipher a message without knowing the key . This is also called "breaking the code" .
 * Brute-Force Attack: Trying every possible key until an intelligible plaintext is obtained . On average, half of all possible keys must be tried .
2.2 Types of Cryptanalytic Attacks
The type of attack depends on the information known to the cryptanalyst :
 * Ciphertext Only: The analyst has only the ciphertext.
 * Known Plaintext: The analyst has one or more plaintext-ciphertext pairs.
 * Chosen Plaintext: The analyst can choose a plaintext message and get its corresponding ciphertext.
 * Chosen Ciphertext: The analyst can choose a ciphertext and get its corresponding decrypted plaintext.
Part 3: Classical Encryption Techniques
These are the basic building blocks of encryption, divided into substitution and transposition.
3.1 Substitution Techniques
These techniques replace plaintext letters (or bit patterns) with other letters, numbers, or symbols .
 * Caesar Cipher:
   * How it works: Replaces each letter with the letter standing 3 places further down the alphabet (or any fixed number 'k') .
   * Formulas:
     * Encryption: C = E(k, p) = (p + k) \mod 26
     * Decryption: p = D(k, C) = (C - k) \mod 26
   * Weakness: Easily broken with a brute-force attack because there are only 25 possible keys to try .
 * Monoalphabetic Ciphers:
   * How it works: Uses any permutation of the 26 letters as the key (e.g., 'a' maps to 'q', 'b' maps to 'x', etc.) .
   * Key Space: There are 26! (over 4 x 10^26) possible keys, making a brute-force attack infeasible .
   * Weakness: Easy to break using frequency analysis . The cipher produces a ciphertext that has the same letter frequencies as the original language (e.g., 'E' is the most common letter in English, so the most common ciphertext letter likely maps to 'E') .
 * Polyalphabetic Ciphers:
   * How it works: Uses a set of related monoalphabetic substitution rules, with a key determining which rule to use for each transformation . This flattens the frequency distribution, making it much harder to break.
   * Vigenere Cipher: A well-known type. It uses 26 Caesar ciphers (shifts 0-25), with a keyword determining which shift (key letter) to use for each plaintext letter .
     * The keyword is repeated to match the length of the message .
     * Weakness: Can be broken if two identical plaintext sequences occur at a distance that is a multiple of the keyword length, as they will generate identical ciphertext sequences. This can be used to find the key length .
   * Autokey System: A Vigenere system where the keyword is concatenated with the plaintext itself to create a non-repeating "running key" .
 * Vernam Cipher:
   * How it works: Uses the XOR operation (⊕) to combine a binary plaintext digit (p_i) with a binary key digit (k_i) .
   * Formulas:
     * Encryption: c_i = p_i \oplus k_i
     * Decryption: p_i = c_i \oplus k_i
 * One-Time Pad (OTP):
   * How it works: An improvement on the Vernam cipher . It is "unbreakable" .
   * Key Requirements:
     * The key is random .
     * The key is as long as the message .
     * The key is used only once and then discarded .
   * Why it's secure: The output is random and has no statistical relationship to the plaintext . A given ciphertext can be decrypted into any plaintext of the same length given a different key (as shown in the "mr mustard" / "miss scarlet" example).
   * Practical Difficulties:
     * Generating and supplying huge quantities of truly random keys .
     * Key distribution: A key of equal length must be securely delivered to both sender and receiver for every message .
3.2 Transposition Techniques
These techniques achieve a different kind of mapping by performing a permutation (shuffling) on the plaintext letters .
 * Rail Fence Cipher:
   * How it works: The plaintext is written down in a zigzag pattern (e.g., in 2 rows) and then read off row by row .
   * Example: "meet me after the toga party" with depth 2:
     * m e m a t r h t g p r y
     * e t e f e t e o a a t
     * Ciphertext: MEMATRHTGPRYETEFETEOAAT
 * Row-Column Transposition (Columnar Cipher):
   * How it works: The message is written into a rectangle row by row, and then read off column by column. The order of the columns is permuted (shuffled) based on a key .
   * Security: Can be made much more secure by performing multiple stages (e.g., two transpositions) .
3.3 Product Ciphers & Rotor Ciphers
 * Product Ciphers: Ciphers that use several ciphers in succession, such as a substitution followed by a transposition. This makes the cipher much harder to break .
 * Rotor Ciphers (e.g., Enigma): Machines that implement a very complex, varying substitution cipher using a series of rotating cylinders, each providing a substitution that changed with each letter encrypted .
Part 4: Overview of Modern Symmetric Ciphers
This section introduces the modern ciphers that replaced classical ones.
4.1 Data Encryption Standard (DES)
 * Issued: 1977 by NIST (as FIPS PUB 46) .
 * Algorithm: Referred to as the Data Encryption Algorithm (DEA) .
 * Key Facts: Encrypts data in 64-bit blocks using a 56-bit key .
 * Status: Replaced by AES in 2001 .
4.2 Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)
 * Status: The most widely used symmetric block cipher today, intended to replace DES .
 * Operations: All operations are performed on 8-bit bytes, using arithmetic over a finite field .
 * Key Facts:
   * Block Size: Always a 128-bit (16-byte) block .
   * Key Lengths: Can be 128, 192, or 256 bits (16, 24, or 32 bytes) . The algorithm is named AES-128, AES-192, or AES-256 accordingly .
 * Structure:
   * The 128-bit block is copied into a 4x4 square matrix of bytes called the State .
   * The cipher consists of N rounds .
     * 10 rounds for a 128-bit (16-byte) key.
     * 12 rounds for a 192-bit (24-byte) key.
     * 14 rounds for a 256-bit (32-byte) key.
   * The initial key is expanded into an array of "key schedule words" to provide a unique round key for each round .
 * AES Transformation Functions (The 4 Stages of a Round):
   * Substitute Bytes (SubBytes): A non-linear substitution step . Each byte of the State is replaced by another byte using a lookup table called an S-box . The 8-bit byte is split into 4-bit row and 4-bit column values to find the replacement in the 16x16 S-box .
   * ShiftRows: A simple permutation step . The rows of the State are shifted cyclically to the left by different offsets.
   * MixColumns: A substitution step that provides diffusion . Each column of the State is transformed using arithmetic over the finite field GF(2^8) .
   * AddRoundKey: The 128-bit State is simply XORed bitwise with the 128-bit Round Key (a portion of the expanded key) .
